Key Risk Factors

Understanding the major contributors to heart disease risk

The risk of heart disease increases with age, and men generally have a higher risk than women before menopause. After menopause, women's risk increases to match that of men.

High blood pressure (hypertension) forces your heart to work harder to pump blood, which can lead to heart disease. Both systolic and diastolic pressure readings are important indicators.

High levels of total cholesterol and LDL ("bad" cholesterol) increase your risk of heart disease by contributing to plaque buildup in your arteries.

Smoking damages blood vessels, increases blood pressure, and reduces the amount of oxygen in your blood. Even light smoking significantly increases your heart disease risk.

Diabetes significantly increases the risk of heart disease. High blood glucose levels can damage blood vessels and the nerves that control your heart.

Being overweight or obese can lead to conditions that increase your risk of heart disease, including high blood pressure, high cholesterol, and diabetes.

Studies have shown that a higher resting heart rate is associated with increased risk of heart disease, independent of other risk factors.

A history of stroke or heart attack significantly increases your risk of future cardiovascular events. Proper management and lifestyle changes are crucial.
